Cadet Leadership Journey (High School - Age 21)

Joined Civil Air Patrol as a high school junior with no prior military or aviation experience. Over five years, progressed through all cadet ranks to achieve Cadet Major—one of the highest enlisted cadet grades. This advancement required mastering aerospace education, leadership theory, physical fitness standards, and demonstrating increasing responsibility in squadron operations.

Technical Development Roles

Served in multiple specialized staff positions as part of CAP's technical development program, each requiring one-year commitment and specific functional expertise:

Cadet Aerospace Education Officer (1 Year): Coordinated squadron aerospace curriculum, organized guest speaker events with pilots and aviation professionals, managed educational materials and lesson planning, and ensured cadets met aerospace knowledge requirements for advancement. Developed understanding of aviation principles, space systems, and military aircraft operations.

Cadet Public Affairs Officer (1 Year): Managed squadron communications and public image. Responsibilities included writing press releases for local media, maintaining squadron social media presence, coordinating photography at events, and liaison with community organizations. Learned professional communication skills and how to represent the organization to external stakeholders.

Cadet Leadership Education Officer (1 Year): Oversaw leadership training curriculum for junior cadets. Planned and executed leadership exercises, mentored cadets through rank advancement requirements, tracked leadership progression, and ensured training aligned with CAP national standards. This role required teaching leadership principles I was simultaneously learning to apply.

Operational Leadership Positions

Element Leader & Flight Chief: Supervised small teams of 5-8 cadets in drill, ceremonies, and training activities. Learned foundational leadership skills: delegation, accountability, clear communication, and leading peers. Flight Chief role expanded responsibilities to coordinating multiple elements and managing larger group dynamics.

Color Guard Commander (2+ Years): Led CAP's ceremonial color guard team presenting the American flag at community events, competitions, and military ceremonies. Commanded performances at veteran memorial services, local government functions, and CAP regional competitions. Responsibilities included:

  • Training team members in precise drill movements and flag etiquette
  • Coordinating logistics for off-site ceremony performances
  • Maintaining uniform standards and professional appearance
  • Representing CAP publicly with discipline and military bearing
  • Competing at regional events, earning recognition for performance quality

Cadet Commander (2+ Years, Including COVID-19 Transition): Highest cadet leadership position in squadron. Assumed role immediately as COVID-19 pandemic forced transition to virtual operations—unprecedented challenge requiring complete restructuring of traditional in-person training.

COVID-19 Virtual Leadership Challenges: Squadron demographics (very young cadets and elderly senior members) made technology adoption difficult. Navigated Zoom platform limitations, maintained cadet engagement without hands-on activities, adapted drill and ceremony training to home environments, and sustained morale during extended isolation. Despite obstacles, maintained squadron activity levels and continued cadet advancement programs. Awarded Commander's Commendation for leadership during this period.

Return to In-Person Operations: Led squadron through transition back to normal operations. Rebuilt in-person training routines, re-established facility protocols, integrated new cadets who joined during virtual period, and restored full ceremony and drill capabilities. Continued as Cadet Commander for approximately one additional year, mentoring successor leadership and ensuring continuity.

Search and Rescue Operations

Qualified in two critical SAR roles supporting Oregon Wing emergency services missions:

Mission Radio Operator: Stationed at mission base coordinating communications between search aircraft, ground teams, and incident command. Responsibilities included logging radio traffic, relaying position reports, maintaining communication schedules, and ensuring all teams checked in safely. Required calm professionalism during high-stress real-world emergencies.

Mission Scanner (Aircrew): Airborne observer in back seat of CAP search aircraft. Trained in visual search patterns, grid search techniques, and identifying ground signals from altitude. Participated in multiple real-world search missions for lost hikers, downed aircraft, and emergency locator beacon activations. Required sustained visual concentration, effective crew communication, and understanding of search probability calculations.

Transition to Senior Member (Age 21+)

Upon turning 21 and after military enlistment, transitioned from cadet program to senior member (adult volunteer) status. After returning from initial military training, resumed CAP involvement supporting cadet development from mentor perspective:

  • Assisted with meeting planning and execution
  • Organized training events and activities
  • Provided guidance to current cadet leadership based on prior experience
  • Continued SAR qualification as mission scanner for emergency operations
  • Currently hold rank of 1st Lieutenant in senior member program

Skills and Values Developed

  • Leadership Under Uncertainty: Navigating COVID-19 transition taught adaptability and resilience when traditional approaches failed
  • Youth Mentorship: Teaching and guiding cadets 5-10 years younger developed patience, communication clarity, and teaching ability
  • Ceremony and Protocol: Color guard experience instilled discipline, attention to detail, and professional bearing
  • Emergency Operations: SAR missions demonstrated importance of preparation, communication, and calm decision-making in critical situations
  • Organizational Management: Staff positions taught functional planning, resource coordination, and cross-team collaboration
  • Public Representation: Representing CAP publicly reinforced importance of professionalism, integrity, and service before self

Recognition

Commander's Commendation Award for exceptional leadership during COVID-19 pandemic transition and sustained squadron operations under unprecedented circumstances.

Seven Years of Consistent Life-Saving Donations

Since beginning regular blood donation as a high school junior in 2018, have contributed over 120 platelet and whole blood donations to Bloodworks Northwest. This sustained commitment represents hundreds of hours spent in donation centers and direct impact on hundreds of patients receiving critical transfusions. Unlike one-time volunteer events, blood donation requires ongoing health maintenance, reliable scheduling, and willingness to endure discomfort for strangers' benefit.

Why Platelets Over Whole Blood

After initial whole blood donations (~5% of total), transitioned to platelet-focused donation strategy (~95% of contributions). Platelets are blood components critical for clotting—essential for cancer patients undergoing chemotherapy, trauma victims, surgical patients, and those with blood disorders. Unlike whole blood (donated every 8 weeks), platelets can be donated every 2 weeks due to rapid regeneration, enabling more frequent contribution.

Platelet donation is significantly more involved than whole blood:

  • Time Commitment: 2.5-3 hours per session (108 minutes connected to apheresis machine, plus setup and post-donation monitoring)
  • Apheresis Process: Blood drawn from one arm, platelets separated via centrifuge, remaining blood components returned through other arm
  • Physical Demands: Must remain still during procedure, potential side effects include tingling (calcium depletion), fatigue, and discomfort from extended immobilization
  • Eligibility Requirements: Stricter health screening than whole blood—requires consistent iron levels, platelet count, blood pressure, and overall health

Despite increased time and discomfort, platelet donation provides greater patient impact—each donation can help 1-3 patients compared to whole blood's single-patient use. Choosing this path demonstrated commitment to maximizing community health contribution.

Donation Frequency & Consistency

Established goal during high school: donate every 2 weeks without exception. This schedule represented upper limit of allowable frequency and required disciplined health maintenance (adequate sleep, hydration, iron-rich diet) to maintain eligibility. Continued regular donations through:

  • High school academic demands and extracurricular activities
  • Military enlistment and training schedules
  • Full-time college coursework at Oregon State University
  • Professional figure skating tour with Disney on Ice (coordinating across cities)
  • Multiple relocations requiring identification of new donation centers

Recent years have seen reduced frequency due to scheduling conflicts and distance from preferred donation centers, but commitment remains—still donate when circumstances allow rather than abandoning practice entirely.

Challenges & Persistence

System Migration Data Loss: Bloodworks Northwest transitioned backend systems during COVID-19 pandemic, resulting in loss of historical donation records and milestone tracking. While frustrating to lose documented progress toward recognition milestones (gallon clubs, donor awards), continued donating despite reset counter—reinforcing that motivation comes from patient impact, not personal recognition.

Multi-Facility Donations: Life transitions (moving for school, military, touring) necessitated donations across different Bloodworks locations and sometimes different blood service organizations. This fragmented donation history across multiple systems, making comprehensive tracking difficult. Maintained commitment despite lack of unified record—demonstrating that doing the right thing matters regardless of credit or recognition.

Eligibility Maintenance: Frequent donation requires vigilance about health factors affecting eligibility: maintaining adequate iron levels through diet, avoiding medications that defer donation, managing cold/illness to minimize deferrals, and scheduling around tattoos, piercings, or travel that temporarily disqualifies donors. Each donation represents not just 3 hours in the chair, but ongoing lifestyle choices supporting eligibility.

Patient Impact

Over 120 platelet donations directly supported:

  • Cancer Patients: Chemotherapy destroys bone marrow's platelet production; transfusions prevent life-threatening bleeding
  • Trauma Victims: Major injuries cause massive platelet consumption; emergency transfusions enable survival
  • Surgical Patients: Complex surgeries require platelet reserves to manage bleeding complications
  • Blood Disorder Patients: Conditions like aplastic anemia or ITP require regular platelet support

Each donation potentially helps 1-3 patients. Conservative estimate: 120+ donations = 150-300 individuals directly impacted. Some patients receive transfusions once; others (cancer patients undergoing extended treatment) require repeated support. This network effect means contribution extends far beyond simple multiplication.

Why This Matters

Blood donation is one of few volunteer activities where individual contribution has immediate, measurable, life-saving impact. Unlike many forms of service where outcomes are diffuse or long-term, donated platelets enter a patient's bloodstream within days, directly enabling their survival or recovery. This tangible connection between action and outcome provides powerful motivation to maintain commitment despite inconvenience.

Moreover, platelet donors are relatively rare—most people donate whole blood (if they donate at all), and fewer than 10% pursue platelet donation due to time commitment and eligibility requirements. This makes regular platelet donors disproportionately valuable to blood supply infrastructure. Hospitals rely on consistent donor pools to maintain adequate inventory; donors who establish reliable 2-week schedules become foundational to that supply chain.

Reflection on Service

Seven years of blood donation taught lessons applicable beyond the donation center:

  • Consistency Over Intensity: Small actions repeated regularly create greater impact than dramatic one-time efforts
  • Service Despite Inconvenience: Meaningful contribution often requires personal sacrifice—time, discomfort, schedule disruption
  • Intrinsic Motivation: Continuing after data loss demonstrated commitment rooted in values rather than external recognition
  • Health as Responsibility: Maintaining eligibility required treating body as resource for others' benefit
  • Quiet Impact: Most effective service happens without fanfare—patients never know their donor's name

This work exemplifies servant leadership principle: putting others' needs above personal comfort, consistently and without expectation of recognition.

Volunteer Service Overview

Contributed 235 hours of volunteer service to Greenhill Humane Society across multiple roles supporting animal care, surgical operations, and emergency response. Started as small animal caretaker and progressed to surgical suite responsibilities, demonstrating increasing trust and capability. Service spanned several years with consistent weekly commitment to animal welfare and shelter operations.

Primary Role: Surgical Suite Assistant

Majority of volunteer time spent supporting veterinary surgical operations through instrument sterilization, facility preparation, and post-operative animal monitoring. This behind-the-scenes role was critical to maintaining surgical throughput and ensuring safe, sterile procedures for spay/neuter operations and emergency surgeries.

Instrument Processing & Sterilization: Received used surgical instruments from operating rooms and processed them through complete cleaning and sterilization cycle. Responsibilities included:

  • Initial cleaning and decontamination of blood, tissue, and biological material from instruments
  • Inspection for damage or wear that could compromise surgical safety
  • Proper assembly of instrument packs based on procedure requirements (spay kits, neuter kits, emergency surgical sets)
  • Packaging instruments in sterilization pouches with appropriate indicators
  • Operating autoclave equipment following strict time/temperature/pressure protocols
  • Verifying sterilization success through indicator strip validation
  • Organizing and storing sterile packs with proper rotation to ensure freshness

This work required meticulous attention to detail—improperly sterilized instruments could lead to post-operative infections, while damaged instruments could compromise surgical outcomes. Learned importance of procedural discipline and how small oversights cascade into larger problems.

Facility Preparation & Maintenance: Supported daily surgical operations through morning prep and end-of-day cleaning. Morning duties included preparing sterilization materials, restocking surgical suites with required supplies, and ensuring equipment was ready for veterinary staff. End-of-day responsibilities involved thorough cleaning and disinfection of surgical rooms, proper disposal of biological waste, and restocking for next day's operations. Maintained clean, organized environment essential for safe veterinary procedures.

Post-Operative Animal Monitoring: Observed animals in recovery areas following anesthesia and surgery. Watched for signs of distress, abnormal behavior, or complications requiring veterinary attention. Alerted nursing staff immediately when animals exhibited concerning symptoms (prolonged unconsciousness, labored breathing, unusual vocalizations). This monitoring role required understanding normal vs. abnormal recovery patterns and recognizing when professional intervention was needed.

Small Animal Care Unit

Entry-level position where volunteer service began. Responsible for daily care of rescued small animals including rabbits, guinea pigs, hamsters, and other species awaiting adoption. Duties included:

  • Daily feeding and water provision appropriate to species dietary needs
  • Supervised exercise and socialization time outside enclosures
  • Habitat cleaning and bedding replacement to maintain sanitary conditions
  • Behavioral observation and enrichment activities to reduce stress
  • Documentation of health concerns or behavioral changes for veterinary review

This role taught responsibility for living creatures dependent on consistent care. Missing a feeding or failing to clean properly directly impacted animal health and adoption readiness. Learned to follow structured care protocols and maintain detailed records.

Cat Care & Deep Cleaning Support

Provided backup support to cat care team during high-volume periods or staff shortages. Performed deep cleaning of cat housing areas—a physically demanding task requiring thorough disinfection, litter management, and environmental sanitization. This work was critical during disease outbreak prevention efforts when contamination control became essential to protect entire cat population.

Emergency Relief Support

Activated during regional wildfire emergencies when evacuated residents brought animals to designated shelter points. Oregon's wildfire season creates urgent need for emergency animal housing, feeding, and supply distribution. Volunteer role included:

  • Transporting donated supplies (food, bedding, crates, medications) to emergency evacuation centers
  • Organizing and inventorying emergency supply stockpiles
  • Assisting with temporary animal housing setup at evacuation sites
  • Monitoring supply levels and coordinating replenishment as needed
  • Supporting displaced pet owners during high-stress emergency situations

Emergency response work demonstrated importance of preparedness, logistics coordination, and calm professionalism during crisis. Seeing families reunited with evacuated pets reinforced the community service value of volunteer work.

Skills and Values Developed

  • Procedural Discipline: Sterilization protocols required exact adherence to procedures—shortcuts risk animal health
  • Attention to Detail: Instrument inspection, animal monitoring, and cleaning standards all demand careful observation
  • Compassion Under Stress: Working with sick, injured, or frightened animals requires patience and empathy
  • Teamwork: Coordinating with veterinary staff, fellow volunteers, and shelter management to support operations
  • Responsibility: Animals depend on consistent, reliable care—volunteer commitment directly impacts welfare
  • Crisis Response: Emergency situations require adaptability, clear communication, and focus on immediate needs

Impact and Reflection

235 hours of service supported hundreds of surgical procedures, cared for dozens of small animals awaiting adoption, and assisted multiple emergency response operations. This work reinforced understanding that behind every adopted animal is extensive support infrastructure—veterinary care, cleaning, feeding, socialization, and logistics. Volunteer experience demonstrated how consistent individual contributions aggregate into significant community impact.
