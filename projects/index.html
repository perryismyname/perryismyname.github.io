Project Overview

My senior capstone project represents the culmination of my involvement with Global Formula Racing's powertrain team. Rather than pursuing a traditional classroom-based capstone, I chose to embed myself in GFR's manufacturing and testing workflow to gain real-world production engineering experience. This project bridges academic theory with hands-on manufacturing, quality control, and system integration.

Scope & Responsibilities

My role encompasses the complete production cycle for formula car components:

  • Receive CAD models from the design team (both OSU and DHBW Germany)
  • Evaluate designs for manufacturability and recommend modifications to reduce cost or improve fabrication feasibility
  • Manufacture components using composites, metals, and electronics assembly techniques
  • Conduct quality inspections and dimensional verification against engineering drawings
  • Install components on the vehicle and integrate with existing systems
  • Participate in vehicle testing to validate performance and identify failure modes

Technical Challenges

Design for Manufacturing: Formula SAE designs prioritize performance and weight reduction, sometimes at the expense of manufacturability. My role involves identifying where tolerances can be relaxed, materials substituted, or geometries simplified without compromising function. This requires understanding both the design intent and the limitations of available manufacturing processes.

Multi-Material Fabrication: Projects span composites layup (carbon fiber, fiberglass), precision metal machining (aluminum brackets, steel fasteners), and electronics assembly (wiring harnesses, sensor integration). Each material system demands different skills, tooling, and quality control methods.

International Collaboration: Working with DHBW Germany introduces logistical complexity—parts designed overseas must be manufacturable with OSU's equipment, and communication across time zones requires clear documentation and proactive problem-solving.

Learning Objectives

This capstone develops skills directly applicable to production engineering roles:

  • Design for Manufacturing (DFM) principles and cost-conscious engineering decisions
  • Multi-process manufacturing (composites, machining, assembly)
  • Quality assurance and inspection procedures
  • System integration and troubleshooting in complex electromechanical systems
  • Cross-functional communication with designers, testers, and international partners
  • Project documentation and engineering change management

Current Status

Project is ongoing through Winter and Spring 2026 terms. Specific components, test results, and final deliverables will be added as work progresses.

Project Background

The DMC2 Mini is a desktop CNC mill kit designed for precision metalworking in a compact footprint. Rather than purchasing a turnkey machine, I chose to build from a kit to deeply understand every mechanical and electrical system. This hands-on assembly process taught me how linear motion systems, closed-loop servo control, spindle dynamics, and machine rigidity contribute to final part accuracy.

Technical Specifications

  • Working Envelope: 12" (X) × 7" (Y) × 5.5" (Z)
  • Spindle: 3 HP (2.2kW), 24,000 RPM with ER20 collet system
  • Motion System: Closed-loop servo motors on precision ball screws and linear rails
  • Resolution: 0.0014mm theoretical (microstepping dependent)
  • Achieved Tolerance: ±0.01mm on aluminum and mild steel with proper finishing passes
  • Controller: Mach3 with custom configuration for servo tuning
  • Features: Automatic XYZ probing, central lubrication system, 40 PSI high-pressure coolant, internal chip management

Build & Tuning Process

Assembly (Weeks 1-3): Kit assembly required precise alignment of linear rails, ball screw installation, electrical wiring of closed-loop servo drives, spindle mounting, and coolant system integration. Each component's installation directly impacts final accuracy—rail misalignment or loose fasteners translate to positioning errors.

Initial Testing & Troubleshooting (Weeks 4-8): First test cuts revealed several issues: servo tuning was too aggressive (causing oscillation), spindle tramming was off (producing tapered parts), and work holding wasn't rigid enough (causing chatter). Systematic troubleshooting involved adjusting PID parameters in Mach3, re-tramming the spindle with a dial indicator, upgrading vise clamping, and experimenting with feeds/speeds for different materials.

Precision Tuning (Months 3-6): Achieving ±0.01mm tolerance required iterative refinement: backlash compensation in software, mechanical preload adjustment on ball screws, thermal management (machine warm-up cycles), tool runout minimization, and developing finishing pass strategies. Each improvement was validated through test part measurement using calipers and micrometers.

Materials & Applications

The DMC2 Mini excels at aluminum and mild steel, with tested success on 6061-T6 aluminum alloys, 1018 mild steel, brass, and various plastics. Typical projects include custom parts for engineering projects, precision jigs and fixtures, replacement parts for equipment repair, and prototype components for design validation.

Key Learning Outcomes

  • Machine Dynamics: How rigidity, vibration damping, and thermal stability affect machining accuracy
  • Servo Control Systems: PID tuning, closed-loop feedback, and stall detection implementation
  • G-Code Programming: CAM software (Fusion 360) workflow and post-processor customization for Mach3
  • Feeds & Speeds Optimization: Balancing material removal rate, tool life, and surface finish
  • Troubleshooting Methodology: Systematic diagnosis of mechanical vs. electrical vs. software issues
  • Metrology: Precision measurement techniques and tolerance verification procedures

Business Application

This CNC mill serves as the primary manufacturing tool for my LLC, which focuses on precision component manufacturing and custom fabrication services. The ability to produce parts in-house enables rapid prototyping, cost-effective small-batch production, and direct control over quality and lead times.

Upcoming Improvements

Current plans include: integrating a fourth-axis rotary table for complex geometries, upgrading to flood coolant for improved chip evacuation, and exploring alternative controllers with advanced features.

Project Context

As part of Introduction to Design (ME 382), our class collaboratively designed and built a multi-stage Rube Goldberg machine where each student team contributed one subsystem. My subsystem—Step #2 in the assembly—receives input from the previous team's ball launcher and delivers output to trigger the next stage. This project required applying the complete engineering design process: problem definition, stakeholder analysis, concept generation, functional modeling, CAD development, physics-based simulation, prototyping, testing, and iterative refinement.

Subsystem Design: Servo-Activated Zipline Mechanism

The final design uses a servo motor to release a weighted basket that travels down a 24-inch zipline, converting potential energy into kinetic energy to trigger the next stage. The mechanism consists of:

  • Input: Redirecting cone guides incoming ball onto a trigger button
  • Actuation: Button press activates servo motor that releases basket latch
  • Energy Transfer: 0.5 lb basket accelerates down angled zipline under gravity
  • Output: Basket impact depresses second button, triggering next subsystem

Engineering Design Process (Four Phases)

Phase 1 - Problem Definition & Stakeholder Analysis: Researched existing zipline and gravity-driven mechanisms, identified stakeholder needs (reliability, timing, safety, ease of reset), and developed measurable design requirements. Analyzed similar Rube Goldberg subsystems to understand failure modes and success factors.

Phase 2 - Concept Generation & Selection: Generated multiple design alternatives through functional decomposition and morphological analysis. Compared concepts using a decision matrix weighted by stakeholder priorities. Developed House of Quality to link customer needs to engineering specifications. Selected servo-activated zipline for optimal balance of reliability, controllability, and mechanical simplicity.

Phase 3 - Detailed Design & Validation: Created full CAD assembly in SolidWorks with motion studies to verify clearances and travel paths. Performed physics calculations to determine required zipline angle, basket mass, and release height for consistent triggering. Built physical prototype and conducted iterative testing to validate simulations and refine trigger sensitivity.

Phase 4 - Testing, Evaluation & Reflection: Collected quantitative performance data across multiple test runs. Analyzed failure modes and success rates. Gathered peer feedback and expert evaluation. Documented lessons learned and provided specific recommendations for future design improvements.

Skills Developed

  • Structured engineering design methodology from problem definition through validation
  • Stakeholder analysis and requirements development
  • Concept generation techniques (functional decomposition, morphological charts)
  • Decision-making frameworks (decision matrices, House of Quality)
  • CAD modeling and motion simulation for design validation
  • Physics-based analysis of mechanical systems (kinematics, energy transfer)
  • Prototyping and iterative testing procedures
  • Technical documentation and portfolio development
  • Cross-functional teamwork and interface management

Project Motivation

This project originated from a practical frustration: streetlights and car headlights disrupting sleep, combined with difficulty waking naturally to daylight. Rather than purchasing a commercial smart blind system, I designed a custom solution that integrated multiple sensors, control modes, and user interfaces to provide flexible automation tailored to my specific needs. This project demonstrates how embedded systems can solve real-world problems through thoughtful hardware integration and control logic design.

System Architecture

The automated blackout blind system uses an Arduino microcontroller to coordinate multiple subsystems:

  • Mechanical System: Blackout curtains mounted in 20×20 aluminum extrusion track (creates light-tight seal), stepper motor with custom axle assembly for wrapping curtains (functions like vertical industrial garage door), limit switch for position initialization and overtravel protection
  • Sensor Inputs: Photoresistor for ambient light measurement, limit switch with hardware debouncing for homing and safety, infrared receiver for wireless remote control
  • User Interface: LCD display showing current mode, position, and status, IR remote for mode selection and manual positioning, ATC timer module for alarm clock functionality
  • Control Logic: Three distinct operation modes with state machine implementation, position tracking relative to initialized home position, moving average filter (5 samples) for photoresistor noise reduction, command deadband to prevent oscillation

Operation Modes

Mode 1 - Automatic Light Tracking: Curtain position automatically adjusts based on ambient external light conditions. As daylight increases, blinds gradually open; as darkness falls, they close. This mode uses filtered photoresistor data with a command deadband to prevent unnecessary motor movement from minor light fluctuations (passing clouds, transient shadows).

Mode 2 - Sunrise Alarm Clock: User sets desired wake time via IR remote. At the programmed time, curtains begin slowly rising over a 15-30 minute period, allowing natural sunlight to gradually increase bedroom brightness. This simulates natural dawn and provides gentler waking than traditional audio alarms. The slow ramp rate is achieved through timed stepper motor steps with extended pauses between position updates.

Mode 3 - Manual Control: Direct positioning via IR remote. User can command specific positions, full open, or full closed. Useful when automatic modes don't match immediate needs (e.g., midday nap, projector use, temperature control). Position commands are constrained by software limits derived from initial homing sequence.

Technical Challenges & Solutions

Signal Conditioning - Photoresistor Noise: Raw photoresistor readings exhibited significant noise that caused erratic curtain behavior. Implemented moving average filter (5-point window) to smooth data while maintaining responsiveness to actual lighting changes. Added command deadband (±2% position tolerance) to prevent oscillation from small fluctuations around target position.

Mechanical Reliability - Limit Switch Debouncing: Initial tests showed false triggers from limit switch bounce during motor vibration. Implemented software debouncing (50ms confirmation window) and added hardware RC filter to eliminate spurious signals. This ensured accurate homing without requiring motor shutdown during normal operation.

Critical Debugging - IR/LCD Interference: Most challenging issue: LCD display exhibited constant flickering, garbled text, and severe lag whenever IR receiver was active, despite both subsystems working independently. After extensive troubleshooting (checking wiring, testing alternate pins, reviewing timing diagrams), discovered IR library was using `LED_BUILTIN` (pin 13) for undocumented status updates. This pin shared a timer resource with LCD refresh routines, creating interrupt conflicts. Disabling the hidden IR feedback resolved the issue completely—a lesson in reading library source code when documentation is incomplete.

Position Tracking Without Encoders: System tracks curtain position by counting stepper motor steps from initialized home position (limit switch). Requires homing sequence on startup and careful tracking of commanded vs. actual steps. Software limits prevent overtravel in both directions, with limit switch serving as fail-safe backup.

Information Processing Focus

As a project for Instrumentation and Measurement Systems, emphasis was placed on sensor signal conditioning and data processing:

  • Photoresistor filtering (moving average, deadband logic)
  • Limit switch debouncing (hardware + software)
  • LCD refresh rate optimization (minimizing flicker, managing update frequency)
  • IR receiver data validation (checksum verification, conflict resolution)
  • State machine implementation for mode switching and command prioritization

Skills Demonstrated

  • Embedded Systems Design: Arduino programming, peripheral integration, interrupt handling
  • Sensor Signal Processing: Filtering, conditioning, noise rejection strategies
  • Control Systems: State machines, position tracking, PID-like control with deadband
  • Mechanical Design: Curtain mounting system, motor coupling, light sealing
  • User Interface Design: Mode selection logic, LCD status displays, remote control mapping
  • Systematic Debugging: Isolating complex multi-system interactions through methodical testing
  • Technical Communication: Produced 2-minute demonstration video showcasing functionality

Results & Practical Application

System has been in daily operation for over a year with high reliability. Sunrise alarm mode successfully replaces traditional audio alarms with gentler, more natural waking. Automatic light tracking maintains desired bedroom darkness during evening hours while allowing daylight entry during day. Manual mode provides override capability for specific needs. The project successfully solved the original problem while providing valuable experience in multi-sensor integration and embedded control logic.

Future Enhancements

Potential improvements include: Wi-Fi connectivity for smartphone control and scheduling, integration with smart home platforms (Home Assistant, Google Home), rotary encoder addition for closed-loop position feedback, motorized tension adjustment to prevent fabric sagging, and weather API integration for predictive light-based control.

Project Overview

This project represents two complete design iterations of a large-format diode laser cutter/engraver, progressing from an initial prototype with significant limitations to a refined, production-capable machine. The journey from v1 to v2 demonstrates systematic troubleshooting, willingness to redesign fundamentally flawed systems, and application of lessons learned through failure. The final 3'×3' working envelope provides practical manufacturing capability for signage, detailed engraving, and precision cutting.

Version 1 - Initial Design & Lessons Learned

Design Approach: First iteration emphasized cost minimization and accessibility—all structural components were either 3D printed from custom-designed models or sourced from hardware stores. Linear motion used threaded rod (all-thread from Lowe's) as lead screws with smooth dowel guide rails. Electronics used standard 4-conductor wire throughout. Custom Arduino code written from scratch to handle motion control and laser modulation.

Critical Issues Identified:

  • Mechanical Limitations: Threaded rod drive exhibited excessive friction, vibration during rapid movements, and backlash that prevented precise positioning. Smooth dowels as guide rails lacked rigidity and introduced flex under carriage weight. Overall accuracy was insufficient for detailed work.
  • Electrical Interference: Standard 4-wire cabling created severe EMI issues—false limit switch triggers occurred frequently, stepper motors exhibited erratic movement, and laser power modulation was unstable. Lack of shielding allowed motor noise to corrupt sensor signals.
  • Software Complexity: Custom motion control code became increasingly difficult to debug and optimize. Implementing acceleration profiles, coordinated multi-axis movement, and G-code parsing from scratch revealed the complexity of mature CNC control software.
  • Limit Switch Configuration: Normally-closed limit switches created constant false triggers from electrical noise, requiring extensive software filtering that introduced latency and unreliability.

Key Learning: Sometimes incremental fixes aren't enough—fundamental design choices (screw drive, unshielded wiring, custom software) required complete redesign rather than continued troubleshooting. Recognizing when to start over is a critical engineering skill.

Version 2 - Systematic Redesign

Motion System Overhaul: Replaced threaded rod drives with GT2 timing belt systems. Belts provide smooth, backlash-free motion with minimal friction—ideal for applications without cutting forces (diode laser). Redesigned gantry to use sliding wheel carriages on aluminum extrusion rails, dramatically improving rigidity and precision. New system achieves repeatable positioning well under 1mm, suitable for detailed engraving and accurate cutting.

Electrical System Redesign: Complete rewiring using shielded cables for all stepper motors, limit switches, and laser control lines. Shields grounded at controller enclosure to drain EMI away from signal conductors. Limit switches reconfigured as normally-open to eliminate false triggers from noise (open circuit can't generate false signal). Power and signal grounds properly separated to prevent ground loops.

Software Migration: Abandoned custom code in favor of proven Laser GRBL firmware. GRBL provides mature motion planning, acceleration control, and G-code interpretation with decades of community development and debugging. Paired with LightBurn software for CAM workflow—handles image processing, path generation, and material-specific settings. This combination provides professional-grade control without reinventing foundational CNC software.

Electronics Enclosure: Custom-designed enclosure houses Arduino CNC shield, stepper drivers, power supply, and active cooling system (fan with filtered intake). Enclosure mounts directly to machine frame with quick-disconnect connectors for all external cables (laser module, limit switches, stepper motors). Includes wall power input and USB connection for laptop control. Organized cable management prevents tangling and provides clean, maintainable system.

Technical Specifications (v2)

  • Working Envelope: 36" × 36" (3' × 3')
  • Laser Module: Diode laser (specify wattage if known) with PWM power control
  • Motion System: GT2 belt drive on aluminum extrusion frame with V-wheel carriages
  • Controller: Arduino with CNC shield running Laser GRBL firmware
  • CAM Software: LightBurn for design, path planning, and machine control
  • Connectivity: USB to laptop, shielded cabling throughout
  • Safety: Limit switches (normally-open), emergency stop integration, laser interlock capability

Applications & Capabilities

Detailed Engraving: Photographs, logos, and fine artwork on wood, leather, acrylic, and coated metals. Capable of reproducing grayscale images through power modulation and dithering algorithms in LightBurn.

Precision Cutting: Through-cuts on materials like cardboard, thin plywood, acrylic sheet, and fabric. Large working envelope enables signage, architectural models, and custom packaging prototypes.

Production Work: Small-batch manufacturing for custom products—engraved gifts, branded merchandise, prototype parts. Repeatability and automation enable consistent multi-piece production runs.

Skills Demonstrated

  • Iterative Design Methodology: Recognizing fundamental vs. fixable flaws, knowing when to redesign vs. debug
  • Mechanical Systems: Belt vs. screw drive trade-offs, linear motion design, structural rigidity analysis
  • Electrical Design: EMI mitigation through shielding and grounding, sensor configuration (NO vs. NC switches)
  • Embedded Systems: Microcontroller programming, firmware selection, motion control fundamentals
  • CAD Design: Custom 3D printed components, enclosure design, cable management
  • CAM Workflow: Image processing, toolpath generation, material parameter optimization
  • Troubleshooting: Systematic diagnosis of multi-domain issues (mechanical/electrical/software)
  • Learning from Failure: Extracting lessons from v1 shortcomings to inform v2 success

Current Status & Future Work

Version 2 is fully operational and used regularly for personal projects and small production runs. System demonstrates reliable performance with minimal maintenance requirements. Future enhancements under consideration include: rotary axis for cylindrical engraving (tumblers, pens), camera system for automatic workpiece alignment, air assist for improved cutting and smoke removal, and enclosure with fume extraction for indoor operation safety.

OSU iLabs Machine Shop Certification

Oregon State University's iLabs machine shop requires students to demonstrate competency across manual machining operations before granting unsupervised equipment access. Rather than arbitrary test pieces, qualification projects consist of functional components for a modular work-holding fixture system—ensuring students understand both precision manufacturing and practical tooling design. Successfully completing these projects validates proficiency in mill and lathe operations, precision measurement, and tight tolerance work.

Mill Qualification Projects (3 Parts)

Part 1 - Step Block: Foundation component for work-holding fixture. Required multiple perpendicular surfaces machined to tight parallelism and flatness tolerances (±0.005"). Operations included face milling, end milling of steps, drilling and tapping mounting holes, and deburring. Key challenge: maintaining perpendicularity across all faces while minimizing setup changes to preserve datum references.

Part 2 - T-Slot Nut: Sliding clamp nut designed to fit mill table T-slots. Most demanding part of qualification due to ±0.002" tolerance requirement on critical width dimension—too loose and it wobbles in slot, too tight and it won't slide. Operations included precision slot milling, thread milling or tapping for bolt interface, and careful measurement with micrometers to verify dimensional accuracy. Required understanding of tolerance stackup and how machining sequence affects final dimensions.

Part 3 - Setup Clamp: Adjustable clamping component completing the fixture system. Combined multiple operations: contour milling for clamp shape, drilling and reaming for pivot pin clearance, threading for adjustment screw, and surface finishing for smooth operation. Required coordination of multiple features with proper geometric dimensioning and tolerancing (GD&T) principles.

Lathe Qualification Projects (2 Parts)

Part 1 - Knurled Mounting Bolt: Threaded fastener with knurled grip section for tool-free tightening. Operations included facing, turning to diameter (±0.005"), threading (external threads to class 2A fit), knurling (diamond or straight pattern), and parting off. Knurling presented unique challenge—excessive force can deflect part and damage threads, requiring proper tool setup and feed rate control.

Part 2 - Multi-Tool Handle (6" Knurled Handle): Most complex lathe project: extended turning operations on 6" workpiece with knurled center section and threaded sockets on both ends for interchangeable tool mounting. Required:

  • Precise length control across multiple features
  • Two different thread sizes (internal threads to specific class fits)
  • Extended knurling operation without chatter or deflection
  • Drilling and boring operations for internal threads
  • Maintaining concentricity between features despite length-to-diameter ratio challenges
  • Surface finish requirements for ergonomic grip

This part tested full lathe competency: facing, turning, drilling, boring, threading (internal and external), knurling, and measurement verification with calipers, micrometers, and thread gauges.

Precision Measurement & Quality Control

All qualification parts required dimensional verification within specified tolerances before certification approval:

  • Tight tolerances: ±0.002" (2 thou) on critical fits
  • Standard tolerances: ±0.005" to ±0.010" on most features
  • Measurement tools: Calipers (0.001" resolution), micrometers (0.0001" resolution), thread pitch gauges, go/no-go gauges
  • Inspection procedure: Systematic verification of all dimensions against engineering drawings before submission

Learning proper measurement technique was as critical as machining skill—understanding when to use calipers vs. micrometers, how to account for thermal expansion, and how to interpret tolerance zones on drawings.
